------------------------------------------------------------

STAGE 2: ANALYSIS
------------------------------------------------------------
Argument Structure Diagnosis (identify spans):
  - Core Claim: The main assertion — the primary point the author wants you to accept.
  - Minor Claim: A subsidiary assertion that serves the core claim.
  - Premise: The evidence or reason given to support the claim.

Paraphrasing Initial Understanding:
  - Paraphrase the core claim into the most simple sentence possible.

Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning:
  - Select: [Inductive | Deductive | None of the above]
  - Inductive = specific to general; Deductive = general to specific.

Implicit Assumption:
  - Select: [Complete | Missing Components]
  - If missing (Missing Components), write what is needed as short keywords for the missingComponent field.

STAGE 3: INFERENCE
------------------------------------------------------------
Consequence Prediction:
  - Positive consequences (max 2 keywords/phrases): outcomes if argument is accepted.
  - Negative consequences (max 2 keywords/phrases): risks if argument is accepted.

Primary Domain Affected:
  - Select one: [Personal/Physical behavior | Interpersonal relationships |
    Social/Cultural norms | Organizational practices | Government/Policy/Law |
    Economic systems]

Alternative Possibility Recognition (choose one and provide up to 3 keywords):
  - "...this only applies when ___, ___, ___"
  - "...other factors are more important like ___, ___, ___"
  - "...this misunderstands ___, ___, ___"

------------------------------------------------------------
STAGE 4: EVALUATION
------------------------------------------------------------
Inference Strength (rate 1–3):
  - 3 (Strong): Premises directly and logically lead to the core claim; minimal assumptions.
  - 2 (Moderate): Premises provide some support but require one significant assumption.
  - 1 (Weak): Premises provide minimal support; major logical gaps.

Credibility Factors (select all that apply):
  [Personal bias | Hearsay/secondhand | Emotional reasoning | Limited expertise | None identified]

Logical Fallacy (select one):
  [No logical fallacy | False dilemma | Hasty generalization | Ad hominem |
   Strawman | Circular reasoning | False cause]
  - False dilemma: Presents only two options when more exist.
  - Hasty generalization: Broad conclusion from too few examples.
  - Ad hominem: Attacks the person rather than the argument.
  - Strawman: Misrepresents someone's position.
  - Circular reasoning: Conclusion used as its own evidence.
  - False cause: Assumes causation without sufficient proof.

Trustworthiness (select all that apply):
  [Evidence-based | Logical consistency | Common knowledge | Cannot be trusted]

STAGE 5: EXPLANATION
------------------------------------------------------------
Target Explanation:
  "I identified [Target] as the primary target because:"
  - It is explicitly stated as the conclusion (e.g., "therefore/thus/so").
  - It is repeated or foregrounded through emphasis and evidence selection.
  - It is the core causal claim (X causes Y) that drives the argument's implication.
  - It is stated early and framed with clear evaluative language (good/bad/should).

Reasoning Structure (select one and fill in spans):
  - Specific cases: "Particular case [Span1] → supports broader principle [Span2] → therefore [Span3]"
  - Outcomes: "If we do [Span1] → then [Span2] → therefore [Span3]"
  - Comparison: "Situation A [Span1] is like situation B [Span2] → what is true of B applies to A [Span3]"
  - Indicators: "Observable evidence [Span1] → suggests pattern [Span2] → indicates condition [Span3]"
  - Expert appeal: "Source [Span1] states [Span2] → therefore [Span3] is credible"

Span Role Labels (for the reasoning structure pattern you selected above):
  For each span slot, identify the type of argument component that fills it:
  - span1 (choose 1): major-claim | minor-claim | premise | not-available
  - span2 (choose 1): major-claim | minor-claim | premise | not-available
  - span3 (choose 1): major-claim | minor-claim | premise | not-available
  Use "not-available" if the chosen reasoning structure does not use that span slot.

Inductive/Deductive Template:
  - Deductive: "General principle [Term X] → Specific conclusion [Term Y]"
  - Inductive: "Specific instance [Term X] → General principle [Term Y]"

Evidential Justification:
  - Good evidence (keywords): evidence from the text that supports your inference.
  - Bad evidence (keywords): weaknesses in the evidence.

Trustworthiness Explanation:
  - Explain in short keywords why the claim can or cannot be trusted.

Fallacy Justification (use the appropriate template):
  - No fallacy: Explain why the reasoning is sound.
  - False dilemma: "Option A [Term1] vs. Option B [Term2] (missing other options)"
  - Hasty generalization: "[Term1] is too broad / evidence is insufficient or not representative"
  - Ad hominem: "Attacks [Person/Group] → therefore rejects [Claim] (without addressing the claim)"
  - Strawman: "[Term1/Original position] is distorted into [Term2/Weaker position]"
  - Circular reasoning: "[Term1/Conclusion] is used as evidence for [Term1/Conclusion]"
  - False cause: "[Term1] does not necessarily cause [Term2] (causal link is unsupported)"
